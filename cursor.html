Cursor experiment.

Project overview.

The computer mouse is a widely used accessory, and we usually a standard point cursor. However, it is a doubt whether a point cursor is the most efficient technique that allows users to select a particular target quickly. The project is a controlled experiment to investigate how different cursors, the size of targets, and the number of targets can influence users’ pointing and selection using a computer mouse and find the most efficient combination to do it.

Challenges.

  1. Required coding to automatically run the test.
  2. Needed complicated and precise calculations to minimize errors.
  3. Due to limited time and resources, there were only three participants participated in the study.

Methodology.

Number of Participants: 3
Tools: HTML, CSS, JavaScript, Python, Excel, GoStats
Study Format: Online via Zoom
Data Analysis Method: Three-way ANOVA

The study used three-way ANOVA, which means it consisted of three independent variables and two dependent variables.

The study had 27 conditions and was conducted using a within-subject design. Each condition had 5 blocks, and each block contained 5 trials. The study lasted approximately 30 minutes, and the participants needed to click at least 675 targets to complete the experiment.

Independent variables.

  • Technique: point, bubble, area
  • Size of targets: small, medium, large
  • Number of targets: few, some many

Dependent variables.

  • Time spent on clicking a target, measured in milliseconds
  • accuracy rate of each trial, rounded to two decimals

Hypotheses.

Total of 6 hypothese was made prior to the experiment. 2 hypotheses was made for each independent variable investigated.

- There is no difference in the mean time to complete target selection tasks using bubble cursors vs. point cursors vs. area cursors.
- There is no difference in the mean accuracy rate to complete target selection tasks using bubble cursors vs. point cursors vs. area cursors.
- Less mean time is needed to complete target selection tasks on the targets with larger sizes.
- There is a higher accuracy rate on selecting the targets with larger sizes.
- There is no difference in the mean time to select different numbers of targets.
- There is no difference in the mean accuracy rate to select different numbers of targets.

Results and analysis.

Time spent.

Technique: the bubble cursor was the fastest at 809.56 milliseconds, and the point cursor was the slowest, which was at 946.13 milliseconds.

However, the effect of taking technique and size of targets as a whole was modest. (F4, 8 = 2.759, p > .05)

Number of targets: the fastest mean time was determined to be "few" targets, which was at 843.79 milliseconds, while "many" targets cost them 902.14 milliseconds to complete the clicks.

In terms of technique and the number of targets, the difference was statistically significant (F4, 8 = 5.557, p < .05) due to the difference between "few" numbers of targets with the area cursor and "many" numbers of targets with the area cursor.

Size of targets: the small tasks took participants 1003.86 milliseconds to click. Medium targets were moderate at 825.85 milliseconds, while the large ones took only 784.92 milliseconds.

For the size of targets and the number of targets, result showed the difference was not statistically significant (F4, 8 = 1.817, p > .05).

Accuracy rate.

Technique: , the bubble cursor obtains the highest rate at 0.972, while the point cursor was at 0.910, which was slightly inaccurate than the area cursor at 0.912.

The interaction effect of technique x size of targets was statistically significant (F2, 4> = 4.152, p > .05).

Number of targets: “Few” numbers of targets resulted in the highest accuracy rate at 0.95. The medium level “some” had the lowest accuracy rate at 0.91.

In terms of technique and the number of targets, there was a statistically significant effect (F4, 8 = 13.735, p < .05). This was due to the difference between the point cursor with “some”, “many” targets and the area cursor with “few”, “many” targets.

Size of targets: The accuracy rate on small targets was only 0.898. However, medium and large targets obtained the same accuracy rate at 0.949.

In terms of size of targets and the number of targets, its interaction effect was not statistically significant (F4, 8> = 1.651, p > .05) due to small differences.

Conclusion.

  • The technique and the size of targets contributed significantly to both time and the accuracy rate, while the number of targets only impacted time.
  • The bubble cursor provided advantages in small and dense target environments compare with the other two.
  • The currently wide-used technique – the standard point cursor did not contribute to a good performance.
  • The combination of the bubble cursor, the large size of targets, and few numbers of targets resulted in the best performance in time and the accuracy rate.
